Electrical Resistivity Surveys (VES) for Geotechnical and Environmental Projects in Knoxville

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

A 4-electrode array, a heavy-duty resistivity meter like the ABEM Terrameter, and a couple of steel spikes—that’s the gear we pull off the truck when a job in Knoxville calls for VES. The terrain here, with its rolling hills and limestone bedrock, responds well to Schlumberger and dipole-dipole configurations. We’ve run vertical soundings along the Tennessee River bluffs and out past West Hills, where knowing the depth to competent rock makes or breaks a foundation design. The method injects a controlled DC current into the ground and measures the resulting potential difference, giving us a layered resistivity model of the subsurface without putting a single drill bit in the dirt. For preliminary site characterization in East Tennessee’s karst-prone landscape, it’s often the fastest way to flag anomalies before committing to a full test pit program.

In East Tennessee karst, a resistivity survey often reveals the cavity before the drill bit finds the void—and that early detection saves the project.

Process overview

Knoxville sits at roughly 886 feet above sea level, and the subsurface story changes fast—from residual clays over the Chickamauga limestone to deep alluvial deposits in the First Creek valley. That’s where the resistivity contrast kicks in. A saturated clay lens might read below 20 ohm-m, while a dry limestone pinnacle can spike past 1,000 ohm-m. We process the field data with inversion software to generate a 1D or 2D pseudo-section, matching each resistivity layer to a likely material type. Our field protocols follow ASTM D6431-18 and U.S. Army Corps of Engineers EM 1110-1-1802 guidelines. Because Knoxville averages over 47 inches of rain a year, moisture content—and its effect on apparent resistivity—is something we factor into every interpretation. When we need to verify the geoelectric model with a direct measurement of penetration resistance, we coordinate a SPT drilling campaign at the same location.

Local context

One thing we notice repeatedly in Knox County—soil pipes and incipient sinkholes can be invisible at the surface but show up as a sharp resistivity low on a VES profile. If a contractor pours a footing over a weathered seam in the limestone without checking, the repair bill can hit six figures. The IBC 2021 Chapter 18 and ASCE 7-22 require adequate subsurface investigation; resistivity data alone won’t replace a boring log, but it’s accepted as a screening tool to guide boring locations and reduce the total number of holes. We’ve seen projects near the Holston River where a 20-foot-wide clay-filled cavity was completely missed by a grid of borings but flagged by a 2D resistivity line. Running VES early, before the site grading starts, is just smart risk management around here.

Additional services

01

1D Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

We expand the current electrodes logarithmically while keeping the potential pair fixed, building a resistivity-versus-depth curve at a single point. This is the go-to method for estimating bedrock depth beneath a proposed tower footing or a stormwater basin in Knoxville’s ridge-and-valley terrain.

02

2D Resistivity Imaging (ERT)

Multiple electrodes along a profile, with automated switching to create a continuous cross-section. We use this to track lateral changes—like mapping a karst conduit under a planned road alignment in South Knoxville or tracing a contaminant plume near an old industrial site.

Reference standards

Common questions

What’s the typical cost for a VES survey in Knoxville?

A standard vertical electrical sounding with a short spread (100-150 ft AB/2) typically runs between US$700 and US$1,070. The final number depends on access, number of soundings, and whether we’re running a 2D line. We provide a firm quote after a site walk.

Can resistivity surveys find sinkholes before they collapse?

Yes—that’s precisely why we use them so often in Knox County. A soil-filled cavity or a fracture zone in the limestone shows up as a distinct low-resistivity anomaly. We flag these targets for follow-up drilling or grouting before they become a problem for the foundation.

How deep can you image with VES in our local geology?

With a maximum current electrode spacing (AB) of 400 to 600 feet, we can reliably resolve resistivity contrasts down to 100-150 feet in the Chickamauga limestone and residual soil typical of the Knoxville area. Depth penetration is limited by the array geometry and the background noise from the urban grid.

Do we still need borings if we run a resistivity survey?

Almost always, yes. Resistivity gives us a continuous profile of electrical properties, but it doesn’t provide a physical sample. We use VES to strategically place borings and reduce the total number, but IBC 2021 still requires direct soil/rock verification for structural design.
